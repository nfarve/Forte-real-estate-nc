Travel Nurse Housing: A Guide for Property Owners

Target Keyword: travel nurse housing property owners

If you own a rental property in Raleigh, Durham, or anywhere in the Triangle, you’ve probably heard the buzz around travel nurse housing. With Duke Health, UNC Medical Center, WakeMed, and several other major healthcare systems all within a short drive of each other, the Triangle draws a steady stream of traveling healthcare professionals year-round.

That demand isn’t going anywhere. And for landlords willing to furnish their property and rent it on a mid-term basis — typically 30 to 90 days at a time — it can be a genuinely rewarding strategy. But like any rental approach, it comes with trade-offs worth understanding before you jump in.

Who Are Travel Nurses (and Why Do They Need Furnished Rentals?)

Travel nurses are licensed healthcare professionals hired on short-term contracts — usually 13 weeks — at hospitals, clinics, and care facilities across the country. They relocate frequently for work and typically need a furnished, move-in-ready place to stay for the duration of their assignment.

Hotels are expensive for multi-month stays. Signing a 12-month lease doesn’t make sense when you’ll only be in town for 13 weeks. That’s exactly why furnished mid-term rentals are their preferred option — and why landlords who offer them are in high demand in markets like Raleigh and the broader Triangle area.

The Pros of Renting to Travel Nurses

  • Higher monthly rent — furnished mid-term rentals typically command a 20–40% premium over comparable unfurnished long-term leases.
  • Reliable, qualified tenants — travel nurses are vetted professionals placed by staffing agencies; they tend to be responsible and financially stable.
  • Utilities and rent often covered — many travel nursing contracts include a housing stipend, meaning payment is consistent.
  • Less wear and tear than short-term — mid-term stays mean fewer turnovers and less frequent cleaning than weekly vacation rentals.
  • Steady year-round demand — the Triangle’s healthcare economy reduces seasonal vacancy risk.

The Cons to Consider

  • More frequent turnover than long-term rentals — you’ll re-rent several times per year.
  • Upfront investment in furnishings — quality furniture, linens, and supplies cost money up front.
  • More active management required — turnovers, cleanings, restocking, and bookings are more hands-on.
  • Lease compliance and local rules — HOA policies, insurance, and local ordinances can apply; confirm eligibility before investing.

Is Your Property a Good Fit?

Not every rental is well-suited for travel nurse housing. The best candidates tend to be:

  • Located within 20–30 minutes of a major hospital or healthcare campus (WakeMed, Duke, UNC, Rex, etc.)
  • 1–2 bedroom units or small homes suitable for one or two people
  • Clean, updated, and move-in ready with no deferred maintenance
  • In safe, accessible neighborhoods with easy highway access

Getting Started: A Checklist for Landlords

Property Readiness
  • Property is clean, updated, and in good repair
  • All appliances function and are modern
  • High-speed Wi‑Fi is available (non-negotiable)
  • Washer and dryer on-site
  • Kitchen fully equipped: cookware, dishes, small appliances
  • Comfortable bed(s) with quality linens and pillows
  • Adequate storage space for a multi-week stay
Lease and Logistics
  • Mid-term lease agreement drafted (30–90 day terms)
  • Pet and smoking policies clearly defined
  • Utility setup confirmed (often included in rent)
  • Landlord insurance updated for furnished mid-term use
  • HOA approval confirmed if applicable
Marketing and Tenant Placement
  • Professional photos of the furnished space
  • Listings on mid-term platforms (Furnished Finder, Airbnb for longer stays)
  • Tenant screening process (ID, references, employer confirmation)
  • Clear move-in/move-out procedures
  • Cleaning/turnover process established
